State of the Football '25

Football does not have a unique solution. That is one of the many reasons it is known as the beautiful game. Each club tries to solve the same problem in their own way — different players, different funds, different history. That chaos is the feature.

But there has been a narrative emerging that says otherwise. That the game is converging on one right way to play. That data has cracked it, that the elite have found it, and everyone else is just behind. I looked at all 40 teams and 689 players across the top flight of English and Spanish football to find out if that is true.

All roads...

Run PCA across five tactical dimensions — territory, chance quality, defensive line, press resistance, buildup quality — and 66% of all tactical variance across 40 teams lands on a single axis. It measures how much a team owns a game. At the far right end: Barcelona, Real Madrid, Arsenal, Liverpool, Man City. The best teams in the world, in both leagues, all pointing in the same direction. That looks like convergence. Look at how they get there and it stops looking that way.

Arsenal's press balance measures the gap between turnovers they force high up the pitch and turnovers they concede near their own goal. That figure is +11.2, the highest in the dataset by a considerable distance. They win the ball back aggressively and rarely give it away in dangerous areas, and they do it through the sheer organisational discipline of the whole squad working in concert. Barcelona's press balance is +8.4, but the mechanism is entirely different: 70.6% of their squad regularly operates outside their nominal position, creating space and confusion through permanent positional movement. Atletico Madrid reach the same elite level of chance quality with a press balance of –5.1 and squad fluidity at just 25%, built on defensive structure, concentration, and transition.

The data does not reveal football converging on one answer. It reveals a game in which several answers work simultaneously, each one shaped by what a specific club has available.

Positional fluidity

The strongest relationship in the team-level dataset — stronger than pressing intensity against results, stronger than buildup quality against results — runs between squad fluidity and territorial dominance. r = 0.771. Clubs where players routinely break their positional role own the pitch. The pattern holds across both leagues, across budgets, and across very different styles of play.

The positional question in football has shifted. It used to be: does a club have a number 10? Now it is whether the left back can operate as a midfielder, the striker press like one, the midfielder arrive in the box like a forward. The clubs who have solved that across a whole squad rather than in one player are the ones eating territory. Newcastle at 57% squad fluidity and an xGDiff of +0.31 are the clearest example of a non-superclub doing it deliberately. They have built a positionally flexible squad as an organisational principle, and it gives them territory that their budget would not otherwise buy.

The regression adds a useful finding. In the Premier League, buildup pass completion has a negative coefficient when predicting xGDiff. Patient, tidy passing out from the back does not win games in England. La Liga returns the same result. What the model rewards is dominance of territory and chance quality, not any particular method of achieving it. How each club gets there remains an open question.

The midfield gap

The creativity distribution is where the two leagues pull apart most visibly. The Premier League has excellent midfielders. Cairney at +4.55, Maddison at +4.23, Ødegaard at +3.84 are genuinely good numbers. In La Liga, those scores land somewhere in the mid-upper range of the distribution. Pedri scores +7.86, which puts him almost three full standard deviations above the next best midfielder in England. Luka Modric, aged 39, sits third across both leagues combined on the same metric: progressive passing received, carrying into the final third, and high defensive actions, z-scored against every other midfielder in the dataset. At 39, those numbers still place him among the best in European football.

The same distribution contains Declan Rice at +0.60. Arsenal Player of the Season. £105 million. His score sits precisely at the Premier League median, which says less about Rice than it does about the metric — this composite does not capture defensive organisation, pressing intensity, or what he does to structure the team's shape off the ball. What it does capture is where creative output ends and defensive contribution begins. Rice is deep in the second category, which is a legitimate and valuable place to be. The gap is between that reality and how he tends to get discussed in England, where "best midfielder in the world" is a phrase used without much scrutiny. In La Liga, +0.60 is the median. The Premier League constructs reputations in ways the data does not always support, and this is one of the cleaner examples of that.

Be yourself

Tactical distance from the league average does not automatically produce results. The moderate middle of the uniqueness distribution is a performance graveyard. Southampton, Ipswich, and Leicester each sit far from their league's average tactical profile, but the distance reflects the limitations of their squads rather than any deliberate identity. Their statistical outlier status is a consequence of poor recruitment and results, not a cause of anything.

At the far ends of the distribution, the picture is different. The clubs that are most tactically distinct from their league average are either among the best or among the worst performers, and the direction they sit in tells the whole story.

Getafe are the most interesting team in this dataset. Their press balance is +9.4, higher than Barcelona and higher than Liverpool, from a club that finished mid-table in La Liga. Mauro Arambarri scores –4.23 on creativity, the second-most defensive midfielder across both leagues. Their system is genuinely alien to the rest of La Liga: relentless counter-press, almost zero creative midfield output, a compact and suffocating shape that concedes very little and generates just enough going forward. They have not won anything significant. They have stayed in the division every season in a league where mid-table clubs get swallowed up regularly. That is a deliberate, coherent, and sustained tactical identity.

Barcelona, Real Madrid, Arsenal, and Man City are all highly unique in their respective leagues and all among the best performers. The style differs entirely between them. What each has in common is a prior commitment to a specific identity, built around what their squads make possible, pursued consistently enough that the results follow.

Football is not converging. What looks like convergence at the top of the table is something more specific: clubs that have understood exactly what they are and built everything around it.

Barcelona can play the way they do because they have Pedri and Frenkie de Jong at the centre of everything, Lamine Yamal breaking lines on the right at 17, and a squad so positionally fluid that opposition defensive structures have no stable reference point to organise around. None of that transfers. A specific group of people, at particular moments in their development, organised with clarity and conviction.

Getafe press the way they do because Arambarri and the defensive block around him make that the most coherent version of what they have available. The football is not entertaining. Staying in the division year after year is the evidence that it works.

Newcastle sit somewhere between those two cases. Not a superclub, no historic claim to the elite, but a mid-budget club that has made positional flexibility a genuine organisational principle rather than an accident of squad assembly. The territory it buys them is real and it has not cost them a transfer record to get there.

The next step has nothing to do with finding a new tactical idea — the question is narrower. The clubs getting the most out of their squads are the ones who have stopped recruiting positions and started recruiting profiles. The difference between deciding a club needs a midfielder and identifying that it needs a player whose progressive passing received sits in the 90th percentile, who presses at 1.5 times the positional median, and who can arrive in the box from deep — someone closer to De Paul or Kovacic, whose completeness scores reflect genuine multi-dimensionality rather than specialisation in one dimension. The data is detailed enough to do that now. The analysis has never been the constraint. Translating what the numbers describe into what the recruitment actually delivers — that is where most clubs still fall short.

Every era of football produces a dominant idea that looks, for a while, like the answer. Total football. Tiki-taka. Gegenpressing. Each one shapes the game until the game adjusts around it. What 2024/25 shows is something different: football has never been more analytically understood and never more tactically diverse at the same time. The tools to understand what a club is have never been sharper. What gets built from that understanding is still the hard part, still entirely each club's own problem to solve — and still, clearly, unsolved by most of them.
