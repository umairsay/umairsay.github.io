Football Analytics · Tactical Analysis

State of the Football 2025

Data40 teams · 689 players · La Liga & Premier League 24/25

The Premier League is the most watched football league in the world. It is also, increasingly, one of the hardest to enjoy. Optimised, intense, and built around whoever can press harder and concede less. Winning in the Prem now looks like a problem that has been solved, and the solution is not especially fun to watch. People say this every weekend. This project tries to test whether the data agrees.

La Liga moves at a different pace. Slower, more technical, more willing to let a player try something. Home to FC Barcelona, a club that has spent a century insisting that winning and playing beautifully are the same thing. Whether that holds up under data is one of the things we wanted to find out.

A full season of advanced team and player statistics across both leagues. 40 teams, 689 players, six tactical dimensions, three position groups. The question underneath all of it: what does elite football actually look like right now, and where is the beauty in it?

Tactical distributions

Six tactical dimensions compared across both leagues. Each curve shows how teams in that league spread across that dimension.

The starting assumption was that these two leagues are fundamentally different. On most dimensions, they are not. Possession, territory, defensive line height, and outcome quality overlap so closely you could almost mistake one league for the other.

Six variables were measured to get here. Field tilt: what share of all final-third touches belong to your team. At 50% both sides are even. Barcelona finished at 72%. Outcome quality: the difference between the quality of chances a team creates and the quality it concedes — more honest than the scoreline. Defensive line height: how far up the pitch a team defends without the ball. Buildup quality: pass completion outside the final third, how clean a team is before it reaches dangerous areas. Press resistance: how often a team loses the ball near their own goal under pressure.

Two dimensions break the pattern. Buildup quality varies nearly twice as much in La Liga — a wider range of approaches to getting the ball out of the back. And press resistance is the only dimension with a statistically significant difference between the leagues. The Prem spreads from Arsenal at 15.1 to Crystal Palace at 27.8. Clubs that have arrived at very different answers to the same question: how much ball are you willing to give away in dangerous areas? Crystal Palace, for what it is worth, still had a positive xG difference despite the worst press resistance in either league. So it is not as simple as protecting the ball and winning.

Where the leagues do differ, it comes down to how teams handle the ball under pressure and how they build from the back. Those two things shape everything that follows.

Tactical families

All 40 teams grouped by tactical similarity. The earlier two branches merge, the more alike those teams are.

Four clusters emerge. The dominant possession clubs — Barcelona, Real Madrid, Man City, Arsenal, Liverpool — separate from everyone else early and by a wide margin. That gap at the top is not a small difference. These five teams are operating in a different tactical register to the other 35.

Below them, a large middle cluster mixes English and Spanish clubs in roughly equal measure. Chelsea and Brighton merge almost immediately, near-identical tactical profiles despite very different reputations. Athletic Club and Bournemouth sit together too: sub-50% possession, aggressive pressing, positive results. Two clubs in different countries playing the same game.

Then a lower cluster that skews heavily Prem. Spurs are here, despite 54.6% possession, which tells you something about what possession without purpose looks like in the data. And at the bottom, the structurally limited: Espanyol, Valladolid, Leganes, Nottm Forest, Southampton, Leicester, Ipswich. Both leagues contribute their share of struggling sides. They all look alike.

40 teams in tactical space

Five tactical variables compressed into two dimensions. Distance means difference. The teams at the edges are the ones that committed to something.

Barcelona and Arsenal share a cluster. Spatially they are pulling apart. Same destination, completely different identities. That gap is where the most interesting question in football lives.

Barcelona sits furthest left in the entire dataset. More dominant than anyone. Man City and Arsenal follow but separate vertically — Arsenal high, Man City and Barcelona lower. Same level of dominance, different texture. Arsenal win territory through an extremely high defensive line and relentless pressing. Man City and Barcelona win it through possession and control. Both work at the highest level. The football each produces looks and feels completely different.

Atletico sit at the very bottom, near Spurs and Man Utd. Three very different reputations, but something structural in common: all three achieve what they achieve through compact shape rather than territory. Atletico's conversion gap is +0.71, the highest in both leagues. They do not need to dominate the passing network to create quality chances. Clinical football in its purest form.

Fulham and Girona are nearly on top of each other. A London club and a Catalan club playing functionally identical football. Brighton and Chelsea sit closer to the elite cluster than to the Prem mid-table. Getafe, as always, alone at the top — high defensive line, almost no possession, 4.12 aerial duels per player per 90, nearly double Barcelona's 1.85. A coherent identity built out of a style most clubs would not attempt.

The interesting thing is not the top or the bottom. It is the middle. A thick band of teams, Prem and La Liga mixed together, all occupying roughly the same tactical space. That compressed middle is where the boring lives. Not at the extremes, where identity exists. In the centre, where it does not.

What each league rewards

Standardised regression coefficients. How each tactical dimension relates to match outcome quality, split by league.

In La Liga, building carefully out of the back is fine. In the Prem, it is associated with worse outcomes. The league actively punishes the kind of football that is worth watching.

This is the most important chart in the piece.

In both leagues, territory is the dominant predictor of outcomes. That part is shared. But look at buildup quality. In La Liga it sits slightly positive — playing carefully out of the back does not hurt you. In the Prem, it is negative and significant. Better buildup pass completion is associated with worse outcomes. Teams that try to play out of the back get pressed into errors and punished. Teams that bypass it, go direct, win territory through intensity rather than control, get rewarded.

This explains more than any other finding in the data. It is not that Prem clubs lack the ambition to play attractive football. It is that the league punishes that ambition. The environment has selected against it over time. La Liga does not have this constraint. It permits more paths to winning. That is why one league produces Pedri and the other produces set-piece routines.

The Prem is not boring because teams play the same way. It is boring because the reward structure pushes them toward the same outcomes. That is a different and more important problem.

Player archetypes

689 players mapped by two composite scores, both measured relative to others in the same position. The top right is where the rarest profiles live.

Two scores were built. The creativity score combines how dangerous a player's passes are, how often they receive progressive passes from teammates, and their overall pass completion. The threat creation score combines crossing and passing danger with progressive carrying — how far and how often a player drives the ball forward themselves. Both are measured relative to players in the same position, making comparisons across roles fair.

The top right, high on both scores simultaneously, is mostly yellow. Pedri sits alone at the summit. Highest creativity score of any midfielder in both leagues, threat creation in the top three at his position. He does not lead any single metric. He leads in the combination. Frenkie de Jong and Dani Ceballos are close behind — three players from two Spanish clubs filling space nobody else occupies.

The best Prem midfielders are present. Ødegaard, Maddison, De Bruyne in the upper half. De Bruyne comes closest to the La Liga standard, which makes sense given where he plays. But the density gap in that top-right quadrant is real and consistent across the whole position group.

Óscar Mingueza at Celta is a right back with a creativity score of 5.38, higher than Ødegaard, Maddison, and De Bruyne. His teammate Marcos Alonso scores 3.99, also as a defender. Celta have built something where their back line creates like playmakers. Trent Alexander-Arnold registers as a defender competing with elite midfielders on both axes. Doku is the pure directness outlier on the far right, more progressive carries and penalty box entries than anyone in the dataset. Gavi is listed as an attacker but presses in the opponent's half at 3.42 actions per 90, more than almost any forward in either league. Positions are starting to mean less and less.

Does the system produce the player?

Team territorial dominance vs individual creativity score, split by position. The upward slope is the finding, and it holds for all three.

Players in the most dominant systems average a creativity score of 2.68. Players in the least dominant average -1.38. Much of what we credit to individuals is the environment doing its work.

The more dominant a team, the more creative its players tend to be. This holds for attackers, defenders, and midfielders. The gap is not subtle. Transfer valuations, national team selections, reputations built over careers — all shaped by individual output that is itself shaped by team context. A player at a dominant club looks more creative in this data than a comparable player at a struggling one, partly because they genuinely are, and partly because dominance creates conditions for expression that survival football does not.

Pedri and Frenkie de Jong are both well above the trendline even after accounting for Barcelona's dominance. The system gives them a platform and they go beyond what the platform alone would predict. The credit belongs to both.

Arsenal's players sit close to where the model expects. Ødegaard is the clearest exception. Building a system that reliably extracts this kind of output from its players is its own kind of achievement — a different kind to what Barcelona are doing, but an achievement nonetheless.

The names worth pausing on are the ones who score well while playing for mid-table clubs. Daley Blind at Girona. Bruno Guimarães at Newcastle. Tom Cairney at Fulham. Patrick Dorgu at Man Utd producing a complete score of 5.87 as a defender on a team with a negative xG difference. Cristian Romero at Spurs, creativity of 3.44 in a club the clustering algorithm put in the struggling group. These players carry something the model did not give them.

Creative midfielders by league

Creativity score distribution for midfielders only. The further right, the more creative.

The Prem distribution is tighter and peaks closer to average. La Liga's is wider with a longer tail to the right. Both leagues produce creative midfielders. The highest-scoring ones in La Liga go further than the highest-scoring ones in the Prem. A gap in ceiling, not in count.

Given what the previous chart showed, this makes sense. Creative midfielders need time and space to operate. A league that rewards bypassing them structurally limits what they can produce. La Liga does not have that constraint, so its creative ceiling is higher.

Tom Cairney at Fulham appearing near the top of the Prem's tail is one of the quieter surprises in the data. Fulham under Marco Silva have built a system that rewards passing quality and positional intelligence. The right environment can surface things the market has not priced in. Modric at 39 is still in the top three creative midfielders across both leagues. Some things do not decline on schedule.

Positions are becoming suggestions

Each dot is a team. Horizontal shows what share of players regularly operate outside their nominal position. Vertical shows how much of the game they play in the opponent's half.

The strongest correlation in the dataset. The more players in a squad who regularly go beyond their listed position, the more that team dominates the pitch. r = 0.796.

This came from looking at the data without a specific question in mind. Positional fluidity measures the share of players in a squad who regularly do things their listed position would not predict. An attacker who completes as many progressive passes as a midfielder. A defender who carries the ball forward as often as a winger. A midfielder who scores as frequently as a striker. The correlation between squad fluidity and territorial dominance is 0.796. With match outcome quality it is 0.678. These are the strongest relationships in the dataset, stronger than any individual tactical variable.

Barcelona are the most fluid squad at 70.6%. Seven of every ten outfield players regularly do things their position would not predict. Every single Barcelona defender in the dataset qualifies as fluid. Man City at 68.8%. Liverpool at 60%. Ipswich and Alaves at 0%, Leicester and Las Palmas at 5%.

Arsenal sit noticeably above the trendline. High territorial dominance with positional fluidity of just 12.5%. Only Ødegaard and Merino qualify as fluid among their outfield players. Every other player operates within a clearly defined role. Barcelona let players roam. Arsenal lock them in place. Both dominate territory. The paths could not be more different.

The best teams in both leagues have largely moved past asking players to stay within their position. Defenders who drive forward, wide attackers who win the ball back deep, midfielders who score. These have become the expectation at the top level, not the exception. Except at Arsenal, where the exception is the model itself.

Specialists and hybrids

A combined score measuring how much a player creates, presses in the opponent's half, and carries the ball forward. Shown separately for each position.

Across all three positions, Prem players score slightly higher on this combined metric. The difference is modest but consistent. The Prem produces hybrids: players who contribute across multiple dimensions simultaneously. La Liga produces specialists: players who are often exceptional at their primary role without adding as much elsewhere.

Declan Rice presses more in the opponent's half than almost any midfielder in the dataset while carrying forward and contributing to buildup. That is the Prem hybrid at its most complete. Dani Ceballos at Real Madrid barely presses but creates with a pass completion of 94.4% and a passing threat score that only Pedri and Modric exceed. A different kind of excellence entirely. Neither is better. They are different ideas of what a footballer is.

The midfielder panel makes this clearest. La Liga has a longer tail to the right, driven by a small group of elite creative specialists. The main body of the Prem distribution sits slightly further right. The typical Prem midfielder does more things. The typical La Liga midfielder does fewer things, but occasionally one of those things reaches a level that is hard to find anywhere else. Bournemouth's Alex Scott, pressing at 3.73 defensive actions in the opponent's half per 90, is the Prem model taken to its extreme. Pedri is the La Liga model taken to its extreme. Both are valuable. They are not the same thing.

The complete midfielder

Top 18 midfielders ranked by a combined score of passing threat, pressing in the opponent's half, and progressive carrying. Both leagues.

The top four are all La Liga. Pedri leads by a distance. He is the most creative midfielder in both leagues and also the most complete. He creates, presses, and drives forward. That combination, at that level, is rare.

From fifth place down the list is mostly Prem. Maddison, Ødegaard, Kulusevski, Kovacic, Alex Scott, Bruno Guimarães, Bernardo Silva. The Prem has complete midfielders. Reading this alongside the previous chart, the picture comes into focus: the Prem's typical midfielder contributes across more dimensions. La Liga occasionally produces someone who takes one dimension to a level that puts them in a category of their own.

Alex Scott at Bournemouth ranking tenth is worth pausing on. He presses at one of the highest rates in the dataset while maintaining solid creative and carrying output. The kind of profile that does not attract much attention at a mid-table club, but on this measure he belongs in the company he is in. Two Bournemouth midfielders appear in the top twenty across both leagues. That does not happen by accident.

Dejan Kulusevski ranks eighth. Signed from Juventus for a modest fee, producing at a level that places him among the most complete midfielders in elite European football. James Maddison fifth, a player who spent years at Leicester before the wider conversation caught up. The complete midfielder list is full of players whose quality arrived before the recognition did.

Uniqueness vs performance

Each team's tactical distance from their league's average, plotted against how well they performed.

Among the better-performing teams, being more different correlates with better outcomes at r = 0.769. Among the worse-performing teams, being more different correlates with worse outcomes at r = -0.582. The middle is where mediocrity lives.

This is the chart that ties the whole piece together.

The relationship between tactical uniqueness and performance is a U-shape, and the quadratic fit is statistically significant. Among better-performing teams, being more different correlates with better outcomes. Among worse-performing teams, being more different correlates with worse ones. Being distinctive is not enough on its own. You have to be distinctive and good at what makes you distinctive. Barcelona are the most unique team in La Liga and the best. Southampton are among the most unique teams in the Prem and the worst. Both are outliers, in opposite directions.

Getafe are very different from the La Liga average — centroid distance of 3.32 — and their xG difference is slightly negative. Staying mid-table with that profile and those resources is its own kind of achievement, but it shows that identity without quality only goes so far. The clubs that sit at the top right of this chart, Barcelona, Arsenal, Real Madrid, Liverpool, are clubs that found an identity and had the quality to execute it at the highest level. That combination is what separates them from everyone else.

The generic middle — clubs near zero on both axes — is not bad. Fulham, Aston Villa, Rayo, Brentford, Sociedad. Solid, functional, unremarkable. They are not distinct enough to be great and not distinct enough to be catastrophically bad. Fine, in other words. And fine, in a sport that is supposed to produce joy, is the problem.

Where things stand

The Prem is not boring because teams play the same way. It is boring because the league's reward structure pushes them toward the same outcomes. Territory wins in both leagues, but in the Prem, careful buildup is a liability. Teams that try to play out of the back get punished for it. La Liga does not have that constraint, and the result is a wider range of how teams build, how players develop, and what the football looks like over a full season.

That one difference shapes everything downstream. It shapes why La Liga's creative ceiling is higher. It shapes why the Prem produces more all-rounders while La Liga produces more specialists. It shapes why 70% of Barcelona's squad operates outside their listed position while Arsenal achieve similar dominance with 12.5% doing so. Different leagues, different incentives, different football.

But the most important finding is the U-shape. The teams that commit to being genuinely different — and are good at what makes them different — outperform the generic middle. This is not a romantic idea about football. It is what the data shows. Bournemouth force more dangerous turnovers than Barcelona with less than half the possession. Atletico have the highest conversion gap in both leagues without needing to dominate the ball. Athletic Club defend with a high line on 48% possession and make it work. Celta have turned their defenders into playmakers. These are all clubs that found something that was theirs and committed to it.

The safe middle is a trap — not just aesthetically but competitively. The clubs that try to be like everyone else end up performing like everyone else. The clubs that find their own identity, whatever that looks like, are the ones that separate from the pack. Football has always rewarded the clubs that know who they are. The data just makes it easier to see.
