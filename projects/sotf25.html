Football Analytics · Tactical Analysis

State of the Football 2025

Year2024 – 25
Data40 teams · 689 players · La Liga & Premier League
MethodsPCA · Hierarchical clustering · Z-score profiling
ToolsR · tidyverse · factoextra

The Premier League is the most watched football league in the world. Most people who follow it closely will also tell you it has become harder to love. Hyper-competitive, physical, increasingly shaped by whoever can press harder and concede less. That is what sustained competition tends to do to a sport over time.

La Liga moves at a different pace. More varied in character, and home to FC Barcelona, a club that has spent the better part of a century insisting that winning and playing well are the same goal. Whether that still holds up is part of what this project wanted to find out, using a full season of advanced team and player data across both leagues.

The question underneath all of it: what does elite football actually look like right now, and where is the beauty in it?

Are the leagues converging?

Six tactical dimensions compared across both leagues. Each curve shows how the 20 teams in that league are spread across that dimension.

The assumption going in was that these two leagues are pulling apart tactically. On most dimensions, they are not. Possession, territory, defensive line height, and outcome quality all overlap so closely the two leagues look nearly identical. The convergence runs deeper than most coverage of either league tends to acknowledge.

Two dimensions break the pattern. How efficiently teams progress the ball from their own half varies nearly twice as much in La Liga, reflecting a wider range of approaches to building out of the back. And where teams position their defensive block without the ball is the only dimension with a statistically significant difference between the leagues (Levene's test, p = 0.048). The Prem spreads from Arsenal at 15.1 to Crystal Palace at 27.8, clubs that have arrived at very different answers to the same defensive question.

Where the leagues do differ, it comes down to how teams handle the ball under pressure and how they organise without it. Those two things shape what players within each system are able to produce.

Four tactical tribes

Teams grouped by tactical similarity. The earlier two branches merge, the more alike those teams are.

Clustering all 40 teams on their tactical fingerprints produces four groups. The distances between them are as informative as the groups themselves. Barcelona, Real Madrid, Man City, Arsenal, and Liverpool separate from the rest early and by a wide margin. The gap at the top of the chart is the data reflecting what the eye already senses watching these teams week to week.

Below them, a mid-table cluster mixes English and Spanish clubs in roughly equal measure. Below that, a lower-table cluster that skews heavily Prem. And then there is Getafe, completely alone. High defensive line, very low possession, more aerial duels than any other team in the data. They have built something coherent and functional out of a style most clubs would not attempt.

Where 40 teams actually live

Each dot is a team. Moving left means more dominant. Moving up or down reflects differences in how that dominance is achieved. Colours show the four clusters from the previous chart.

Barcelona and Arsenal share a cluster. Spatially they are pulling apart. Same destination, completely different journeys.

The horizontal axis accounts for 66% of all variation across teams. It captures overall tactical dominance, a combination of possession, territory, line height, and buildup quality. Barcelona sits furthest left in the entire dataset, followed by Man City, Arsenal, Liverpool, and Real Madrid. The Prem's lower half bunches toward the right, with modest differences between those clubs.

The vertical axis captures the texture of how a team plays rather than how much it dominates. Arsenal and Barcelona land in the same cluster horizontally but separate vertically. Arsenal achieve their dominance through a very high defensive line and relentless pressing. Barcelona achieve it through possession and control. Both work well. The football each produces looks and feels quite different.

Who is actually doing what

689 players mapped by two scores. Up the chart means more creative through passing and ball receipt. Right means more direct threat through carrying and crossing. Both scores are measured relative to other players in the same position, so defenders and attackers can be fairly compared.

The top right is where players who score highly on both measures end up. That space is mostly yellow, La Liga. Pedri sits at the furthest point, with the highest creativity score of any midfielder in both leagues and a threat creation score in the top three at his position. He does not lead any single metric. He leads in the combination, and that combination is genuinely hard to find.

The best Prem midfielders are present. Ødegaard, Maddison, De Bruyne are all in the upper half. De Bruyne comes closest to the La Liga standard, which makes sense given where he plays. The gap between the two leagues in that top corner holds across the whole position group, not just a few standout individuals.

Trent Alexander-Arnold registers as a defender who scores alongside elite midfielders on both axes. His profile does not fit neatly into his listed position, and the data reflects that. Doku sits alone on the far right, lower on creativity but generating more direct threat through carrying than anyone else in the dataset by a meaningful margin. A different kind of player, a different kind of contribution.

Does the system produce the player?

Each dot is a player. Horizontal shows how dominant their team is. Vertical shows how creative that player is individually. Split by position. The upward slope in each panel is the finding.

The slope is positive across all three positions, and statistically significant in each case. The system shapes what players are able to do.

The more dominant a team, the more creative its players tend to be. This holds for attackers, defenders, and midfielders. Players in the top cluster average a creativity score of 2.54. Players in the bottom cluster average -1.21. The environment a player is in does a significant amount of the work that often gets attributed to the individual alone.

Pedri and Frenkie de Jong are both well above the trendline even after accounting for how dominant Barcelona are. The system gives them a platform and they go beyond what the platform alone would predict. In their case the credit belongs to both.

Arsenal's players sit close to where the model would expect for a team at their level of territorial dominance. Ødegaard is the clearest exception. Building a system that consistently gets this kind of output from its players is its own kind of achievement.

Some of the more interesting names are the ones who score well on creativity while playing for mid-table clubs. Daley Blind at Girona. Bruno Guimarães at Newcastle. Players whose output the model did not predict from their surroundings.

Creative midfielders by league

Creativity score distribution for midfielders only. The further right, the more creative.

Looking at midfielders on their own, a clear pattern emerges. The Prem distribution is tighter, peaking closer to average. La Liga's is wider with a longer tail to the right. Both leagues produce creative midfielders. The highest-scoring ones in La Liga go further than the highest-scoring ones in the Prem. A gap in ceiling more than count.

Tom Cairney at Fulham appearing near the top of the Prem's tail is one of the more unexpected findings in the data. His creativity score places him alongside players with a much higher profile. Fulham under Marco Silva have built a system that rewards the kind of passing quality and positional awareness this score captures. The right environment can surface things the market has not yet priced in.

Positions are becoming suggestions

Each dot is a team. Horizontal shows what share of players regularly operate outside their nominal position. Vertical shows how much of the game they play in the opponent's half.

The strongest correlation in the dataset. The more players in a squad who regularly go beyond their listed position, the more that team dominates the pitch. r = 0.796.

This came from looking at the data without a specific question in mind. Squads where players regularly do things their listed position would not predict correlate with territorial dominance at r = 0.796, and with outcome quality at r = 0.678. Barcelona are the most fluid squad in both leagues at 70.6%, followed by Man City at 68.8% and Liverpool at 60%. Ipswich and Leicester anchor the other end.

Arsenal sit noticeably above the trendline. They achieve high territorial dominance with positional fluidity of around 12.5%, with every player operating within a clearly defined and tightly organised role. A different path to a similar outcome.

The best teams in both leagues have largely moved away from asking players to stay narrowly within their position. Defenders who drive forward, wide attackers who win the ball back deep, midfielders who contribute goals. These have become the expectation at the top level rather than the exception.

Two different ideas of what a footballer is

A combined score measuring how much a player creates, presses in the opponent's half, and carries the ball forward. Shown separately for each position. The dashed lines mark each league's average.

Across all three positions, Prem players score slightly higher on this combined metric than La Liga players. The difference is modest but consistent. The Prem tends to produce players who contribute across multiple dimensions simultaneously. La Liga tends to produce players who are more specialised, often exceptional at their primary role without adding much elsewhere.

The Prem has a reputation for physicality, La Liga for technical quality. What the data shows is more nuanced than either reputation. The Prem demands a broader range of contributions from each player. La Liga permits a deeper form of specialisation. Both approaches develop strong footballers. They develop different ones.

The midfielder panel makes this clearest. La Liga has a longer tail to the right, driven by a small group of elite creative midfielders. The main body of the Prem distribution sits slightly further right. The typical Prem midfielder contributes across more dimensions. The typical La Liga midfielder focuses on fewer things, but occasionally one of those things reaches a level that is hard to find anywhere else.

The complete midfielder

Top 18 midfielders ranked by a combined score of passing threat, pressing in the opponent's half, and progressive carrying. Both leagues.

The top four are all La Liga. Pedri leads by a distance. He scores highest on a metric that combines creative output, pressing in the opponent's half, and forward ball-carrying. He is the most creative midfielder in both leagues and also the most complete. He creates, presses, and drives forward. That combination, at that level, is rare.

From fifth place down the list is mostly Prem. Maddison, Ødegaard, Kulusevski, Kovacic, Bruno Guimarães, Bernardo Silva. The Prem has complete midfielders. Reading these two charts together, the picture comes into focus: the Prem produces players who contribute across the board, while La Liga occasionally produces someone who takes one dimension to a level that puts them in a category of their own.

Alex Scott at Bournemouth ranking tenth is worth pausing on. He presses at one of the highest rates in the dataset for a midfielder, while maintaining solid creative and carrying output. The kind of profile that does not attract much attention at a mid-table club, but on this measure he belongs in the company he is in.

Where things stand

Looking at both leagues through this data, what comes through is a picture of two different approaches to the same game. The Premier League has optimised for competitiveness and delivered exactly that, a league where any team can beat any other on a given day and where the margins at the top are genuinely small. La Liga has allowed more tactical variety, produced more of a specific kind of player, and at the top end is home to a club that has been making the same argument about football for decades and keeps finding ways to win with it.

Those are not the only two options. Most clubs are working with something more modest. Not every squad can sustain a high press across a full season. Not every club has the conditions to develop a generation of players through the same philosophy. The more interesting question, for most, is what you do with what you have.

Getafe are one answer the data did not anticipate. High defensive line, almost no possession, more aerial duels than any team in the dataset. Their profile is so distinct that no clustering method knows where to place them. They sit alone, separated from every other team. In a landscape where most clubs are converging on similar solutions, a clear and committed identity of your own turns out to matter.

The data also shows that systems, however well built, do not fully account for individuals. Some players produce well above what their environment would predict. Some clubs find ways to compete that the numbers would not have suggested going in. There is still room in this game for something the model did not expect. That much, at least, the data is clear about.
