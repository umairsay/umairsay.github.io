Football Analytics · Tactical Analysis

State of the Football 2025

Year2024 – 25
Data40 teams · 689 players · La Liga & Premier League
MethodsPCA · Hierarchical clustering · Z-score profiling
ToolsR · tidyverse · factoextra

Football got optimized. Over the last decade, clubs gained access to data that could tell them exactly how to reduce uncertainty — press higher, defend deeper, minimize variance, repeat. The Premier League, the richest and most competitive league in the world, embraced this faster and more completely than anywhere else. The result is a league where any team can beat any other on a given day, where the margins are razor thin, and where most matches feel like they are being played by clubs running the same algorithm.

It is the most competitive league in the world. It is also, increasingly, one of the least beautiful to watch.

La Liga has resisted some of this. It moves at a different pace, tolerates more variety, and at the top end is home to FC Barcelona — a club that has spent the better part of a century insisting that winning and playing well are the same goal. Whether that idea still holds up, and what it means for where football is headed, is what this project set out to find out. Using a full season of advanced team and player data across both leagues, 40 teams and 689 players, here is what the numbers say.

Are the leagues converging?

The two leagues measured across six tactical dimensions. Most of what you would expect to find different turns out to be the same.

The first question is whether the two leagues are actually as different as the conversation around them suggests. Six variables were chosen to capture the most important tactical dimensions. Field tilt measures what share of all final-third touches belong to your team — not just who has the ball, but where the game is actually being played. A score of 50% means both teams are equally active in dangerous areas. Barcelona finished the season at 72%. Outcome quality measures the difference between the chances a team creates and the chances it concedes, a more honest measure of performance than the scoreline. Defensive line height tells you how far up the pitch a team organises without the ball. Buildup quality measures pass completion outside the final third. Press resistance measures how often a team turns the ball over in dangerous areas near their own goal under pressure.

The finding is not what most people would expect. On most of these dimensions, the two leagues are nearly identical. Possession, territory, defensive line height, outcome quality — the curves overlap so closely you could almost swap the labels. The tactical convergence is real and it runs deeper than most coverage of either league acknowledges. The data revolution did not just change how the Prem plays. It changed how football in general plays, and the result is two leagues that look more alike than they ever have.

Two dimensions break the pattern. Buildup quality varies nearly twice as much in La Liga, suggesting a wider range of philosophies around how to move the ball out of the back. And press resistance is the only dimension with a statistically significant difference between the leagues (Levene's test, p = 0.048). The Prem spreads from Arsenal at 15.1 to Crystal Palace at 27.8 — a gap that reflects a league that has not settled on a single answer to the question of how much of the ball it is acceptable to lose in dangerous areas. Some clubs press and win it back high. Others sit deeper and accept the risk. La Liga is more uniform here, which is interesting on its own.

The story these six panels tell together is this: the leagues have converged on the surface. Where they still differ is in how teams handle adversity — under pressure, without the ball, in their own half. Those differences, small as they look on the chart, shape everything about the kind of players each league produces.

Four tactical tribes

Forty teams, four groups. The elite separate from everyone else early, and by a wide margin.

If the leagues are more similar than expected in aggregate, are there still meaningful differences between individual clubs? This chart groups all 40 teams by how tactically similar they are across those same five variables. Teams that branch together low on the vertical axis are very alike. Teams that only connect high up are very different. The height of the join is a measure of distance.

Four clusters emerge, and the distances between them matter as much as the groups themselves. Barcelona, Real Madrid, Man City, Arsenal, and Liverpool sit completely apart from the other 35 teams. The gap at the top of the chart is not subtle. These clubs are not just better — they are doing something categorically different to everyone else in both leagues. They are not playing a more optimized version of average football. They have left average football behind entirely.

Below them, a mid-table cluster mixes English and Spanish clubs in roughly equal proportion — average football, it turns out, looks broadly the same regardless of which country it comes from. Below that, a lower-table cluster that skews heavily Prem. Each group is a snapshot of where clubs currently sit in the game's ongoing sorting process: those who have found something, those still searching, and those for whom the current model is not working at all.

Where 40 teams actually live

A map of tactical space across both leagues. Distance means difference. The vertical axis is where the real story lives.

Barcelona and Arsenal share a cluster. Spatially they are pulling apart. Same destination, completely different journeys.

The dendrogram groups teams. This chart maps where they actually sit in relation to each other. Principal component analysis compresses all five tactical variables into two axes that explain the most variation across teams — think of it as a map where distance means difference. The horizontal axis captures 66% of all variation and maps onto overall tactical dominance. Moving left means more dominant. Barcelona sits furthest left in the entire dataset. Man City, Arsenal, Liverpool, and Real Madrid follow. The Prem's lower half bunches toward the right, tactically similar enough that the gap between those clubs is modest.

The vertical axis is where the real story lives. It captures not how dominant a team is but how they achieve that dominance. Arsenal and Barcelona occupy the same cluster horizontally — both are among the most territorially dominant teams in Europe — but they separate sharply on the vertical axis, and that separation tells you everything about what kind of football they play.

Arsenal sit high on this axis. Their system is built around one of the most aggressive defensive lines in European football. That high line compresses the space opponents have to work in, triggers turnovers in dangerous areas, and enables coordinated pressing waves that manufacture chaos on the opponent's side of the pitch. It is a system designed to make the opposition uncomfortable at all times. Arteta has taken what was historically the Prem's defining characteristic — intensity, physicality, urgency — and built it into something more sophisticated and more extreme than any Prem club has done before.

Barcelona sit lower. Their approach inverts Arsenal's logic entirely. Rather than pressing to win the ball back, they keep it away from danger in the first place. At 72% field tilt the opposition rarely got close enough to their goal to threaten it in any meaningful way. Domination through suffocation, not aggression. The ball is their shield as much as their weapon. One system is a trap. The other is a stranglehold. Both produce the same result on the horizontal axis — dominance — while producing completely different football to watch.

What the rest of the map reveals is equally important. The dense cluster in the middle reflects clubs from both leagues that have not yet committed to a clear identity. They press some of the time, sit deep some of the time, try to possess some of the time. In individual matches this can look like tactical flexibility. In aggregate across a full season it averages out into sameness. And the data is clear: sameness does not dominate. The clubs at the edges of this map — the ones furthest from the crowded middle — are there because they made a real choice about who they are and built everything around it.

This is where the data starts to say something that goes beyond tactics. Barcelona's identity was not built from a manual or a model. It came from a philosophy about what football should look like — technical mastery, collective movement, respect for the ball, individual expression within a shared structure. The players who come through La Masia absorb this before they absorb anything else. It is instinct before it is instruction. When Pep Guardiola brought elements of that to the Premier League, clubs across the league spent years attempting to copy the outputs — the high line, the pressing triggers, the positional structures — while missing what produced them. The middle of the map is crowded because the Prem learned the answers without learning the question. The result is a league of tactical mimicry that looks very organised and feels very similar from week to week.

The teams that stand out in this data are the ones that stopped copying. That is as true of the clubs at the far left as it is of any club sitting apart from the pack. Tactical identity, knowing what kind of football you want to play and building everything around that conviction, turns out to be one of the strongest predictors of success in the data. Not budget. Not any single metric. Identity.

Who is actually doing what

Every player in both leagues plotted by two scores. The top right is where the most interesting players live.

Moving from teams to players, the question becomes: what does tactical identity at the club level actually produce in the individuals who play within it? Two composite scores were built. The creativity score combines how dangerous a player's passes are on average, how often they receive progressive passes from teammates, and their overall pass completion — together capturing how much a player moves the ball forward in a controlled and dangerous way. The threat creation score combines passing and crossing threat with progressive carrying, how far and how often a player drives the ball forward themselves. Critically, both scores are calculated relative to other players in the same position. A defender who passes well for a defender scores as high as a midfielder who passes well for a midfielder. The comparison is fair across roles.

The top right is where players who score highly on both measures end up — creative and threatening simultaneously. That space is mostly yellow. La Liga. Pedri sits at the furthest point. The highest creativity score of any midfielder in both leagues, and a threat creation score in the top three at his position. He does not lead any single metric. He leads in the combination. What makes him genuinely unusual is not that he is elite at one thing — it is that being elite at one reinforces rather than limits the other. Most players who score this high on creativity do so by sacrificing carrying threat. Pedri does not make that trade.

The best Prem midfielders are present — Ødegaard, Maddison, De Bruyne in the upper half. De Bruyne comes closest to the La Liga standard, which makes sense given where he plays. The gap between the two leagues in that top corner is not a story about exceptional individuals. It reflects something structural about what each league's systems allow its midfielders to become. Systems that protect and create space produce creative players. Systems that demand defensive contribution first tend to produce something more complete but less exceptional.

Trent Alexander-Arnold registers as a defender who scores alongside elite midfielders on both axes. His profile does not fit his listed position, and the data makes that undeniable. Doku sits alone on the far right — lower on creativity but generating more direct threat through carrying than anyone else in the dataset. A completely different kind of contribution to any other player on the chart, and a reminder that not all of the most interesting profiles fit neatly onto a single spectrum.

Does the system produce the player?

Team dominance vs individual creativity, split by position. The upward slope in each panel is the finding.

Players in the most dominant systems average a creativity score of 2.54. Players in the least dominant average -1.21. The gap between those numbers is largely the system.

The previous chart raises an obvious question. Are creative players simply the product of creative systems? If you play for a dominant team that controls territory and protects space, does the environment explain your individual output? This chart tests it directly. The horizontal position of each dot shows how dominant their team is by field tilt. The vertical position shows how creative that individual player is. Three panels separate the relationship by position.

The answer is yes, substantially, and it holds across attackers, defenders, and midfielders alike. The more dominant a team, the more creative its players tend to be. The gap between the top and bottom clusters — 2.54 versus -1.21 — is large. Transfer valuations, reputations, national team selections: all of these are shaped by individual output that is itself shaped by team context. When a player moves from a mid-table club to an elite one and suddenly looks like a different player, this is usually why. The system was always doing more of the work than the price tag acknowledged.

Pedri and Frenkie de Jong are both well above the trendline even after accounting for how dominant Barcelona are. The system gives them a platform and they exceed what the platform alone would predict. The credit belongs to both. Arsenal's players sit close to where the model expects for a team at their level of dominance. Ødegaard is the clearest exception — a player who consistently produces above what the system alone would account for.

The most interesting names on this chart are the ones who score well on creativity despite playing for mid-table clubs. Daley Blind at Girona. Bruno Guimarães at Newcastle. Players whose output the model did not predict from their surroundings. The system explains a lot. It does not explain everything, and the players it cannot explain tend to be the ones worth watching most closely.

Creative midfielders by league

Creativity score distribution for midfielders only. Both leagues, same axis. The shape of the curve matters as much as the average.

One of the most debated claims in football is that La Liga produces better creative midfielders. This chart isolates midfielders from both leagues and compares their distributions directly. A narrow, tall curve means most players cluster around a similar value. A wide, flat curve with a long tail means more spread — more players at the extremes in both directions.

The Prem distribution is tighter, peaking closer to average. La Liga's is wider with a longer tail to the right. The claim holds — but only at the ceiling. Both leagues produce creative midfielders in similar numbers. La Liga produces more of them at the very top end. And given what the previous chart showed about systems producing players, this is not primarily a talent story. It is a systems story. La Liga preserves the conditions for elite creative midfielders to operate at their ceiling. The Prem, increasingly, does not.

Tom Cairney at Fulham appearing near the top of the Prem's tail is one of the more genuinely unexpected findings in the data. His creativity score places him alongside players with a much higher profile and a much higher price tag. Fulham under Marco Silva have built a system that protects exactly the kind of passing quality and positional intelligence this score captures. The right environment surfaces things the market has not yet priced in.

Positions are becoming suggestions

Squad fluidity vs territorial dominance. One dot per team. The trendline is the finding.

The strongest correlation in the dataset. The more players in a squad who regularly go beyond their listed position, the more that team dominates the pitch. r = 0.796.

This finding was not the result of testing a hypothesis. It emerged from looking at the data without a predetermined conclusion. Positional fluidity measures the share of players in a squad who regularly do things their listed position would not predict — an attacker completing as many progressive passes as a midfielder, a defender carrying the ball forward as often as a winger, a midfielder scoring as frequently as a striker. For each position, a player qualifies as fluid if they exceed 1.5 times the median value for that metric in their position group. The question was simply: does this correlate with anything interesting?

The answer was stronger than expected. The correlation between squad fluidity and field tilt is 0.796. With match outcome quality it is 0.678. These are the strongest relationships in the entire dataset, stronger than possession, stronger than defensive line height, stronger than any individual tactical variable measured. Barcelona are the most fluid squad in both leagues at 70.6%, followed by Man City at 68.8% and Liverpool at 60%. Ipswich and Leicester anchor the bottom at 0% and 5%. The relationship holds across both leagues and across every level of the table.

Arsenal are the notable exception, sitting well above the trendline. High territorial dominance, low positional fluidity — around 12.5%. Every player operates within a clearly defined role inside a tightly organised system. It works. But Arsenal are an outlier in both leagues on this measure, not a model. The general pattern across 40 teams is strong and consistent: squads where players regularly transcend their position dominate the pitch.

What this tells us about where football is going is significant. The clubs at the top are not built around players who do their position well. They are built around players who do their position and then some. A central defender who drives into midfield. A wide attacker who presses from the front and wins the ball back forty metres from goal. A midfielder who finishes like a striker. These are not special cases at the top level anymore. They are the baseline. The clubs still asking players to stay in their lanes are the clubs in the middle of the map.

Two different ideas of what a footballer is

A combined score of creating, pressing, and carrying — by position and league. The dashed lines mark each league's average.

The fluidity chart looks at teams. This one looks at individual players and asks: which league produces more well-rounded players? A completeness score was built combining three things. Passing threat — how dangerous a player's passes are on average. Defensive actions in the opponent's half — how much a player contributes to pressing and winning the ball high up the pitch. Progressive carrying — how far and how often a player drives forward with the ball. A player who contributes meaningfully across all three scores high. A player who is extraordinary at one thing but absent in the others scores lower, even if that one thing is exceptional.

Across all three positions, Prem players score slightly higher on this combined metric. The difference is modest but consistent. The Prem produces players who do multiple things at a reasonable level simultaneously. La Liga produces players who are more specialised — often exceptional at their primary function without adding much outside of it. This is not a talent difference. It is a product of what each system demands. The Prem, in its drive to optimize every dimension, has produced a generation of players who do everything adequately. La Liga, in its tolerance of a purer form of individual expression, has preserved the conditions for a player to become extraordinary at something specific.

The midfielder panel makes this tension clearest. La Liga has a longer tail to the right, pulled by a small group of elite creative midfielders. The main body of the Prem distribution sits slightly further right. The typical Prem midfielder is more complete. The typical La Liga midfielder is more specialized. And occasionally, one of those specialists reaches a level that makes completeness feel beside the point.

The complete midfielder

Top 18 midfielders by combined create, press, carry score. Yellow is La Liga, blue is Prem.

The top four are all La Liga. Pedri leads by a distance. On a metric that combines creative output, pressing in the opponent's half, and forward ball-carrying, he is not only the most creative midfielder in both leagues but also the most complete. He does not sacrifice pressing to create. He does not sacrifice carrying to press. The qualities reinforce each other, and the result is a score that separates him from everyone else on the list. That combination, at that level, in a sport that has spent a decade trying to optimize every player into a well-rounded contributor, is genuinely rare.

From fifth place down the list is mostly Prem. Maddison, Ødegaard, Kulusevski, Kovacic, Bruno Guimarães, Bernardo Silva. The Prem has complete midfielders. It does not produce the creative ceiling that La Liga does at the very top. Reading the last two charts together, the picture is clear: the Prem has built a generation of players who contribute across the board. La Liga has preserved the conditions for the occasional player who takes one dimension so far that the board becomes irrelevant.

Alex Scott at Bournemouth ranking tenth is the kind of finding that only surfaces when you stop looking for the names you already know. He presses at one of the highest rates in the dataset for a midfielder while maintaining solid creative and carrying output. At a club nobody is writing essays about, in a league where everyone is trying to win the same way, he is quietly doing something the data recognizes as elite. That is the whole point of this kind of analysis.

Where things stand

Football got optimized, and optimization has a cost. The Premier League is the clearest example of what happens when a sport fully commits to removing uncertainty — you get the most competitive league in the world, with the smallest margins, the most prepared teams, and the most forgettable football. The data confirms what watching the league already suggests: most clubs look broadly similar in aggregate because most clubs are solving the same problem the same way. The tactical convergence is real. The middle of the map is crowded.

What the data also shows is that the teams who have actually pulled away from everyone else are not the ones who optimized hardest. They are the ones who stopped optimizing and started being something. Barcelona at 72% field tilt and 70.6% positional fluidity did not get there by following a model. They got there because the players who come through their system absorb a philosophy before they absorb anything else — technical mastery, collective movement, individual expression within a shared structure. That philosophy is not a tactical system. It is an identity. And identity, according to this data, is the strongest predictor of sustained dominance that exists.

The Premier League copied the surface of what made teams like Barcelona and the early Guardiola sides dominant — the high line, the pressing structure, the positional play — without building the cultural foundation underneath it. The result is a league that has converged tactically without converging on anything meaningful. Clubs that know who they are stand apart in the data. The ones that do not all look the same.

Where is football headed? Both directions at once. More optimization, more convergence, more of the same at the level of the average club. And at the edges — the clubs that have committed to something of their own — something more interesting. The fluidity finding points here. The player profile of Pedri points here. The appearance of Tom Cairney near the top of a creativity chart, or Alex Scott in elite pressing company at Bournemouth, or Daley Blind producing above what his team context would predict at Girona — all of it points here. The game still has room for clubs that know who they are and players who exceed the system they are in.

The game does not need better optimization. It has enough of that. What it needs — what the data quietly argues for — is more clubs willing to be themselves.
