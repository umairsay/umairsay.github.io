Visible Value:
Performance, Popularity, & Pay in MLS

Year2024 – 25
Data440 outfield players, 2024 MLS season
MethodsFixed-effects panel regression
SourcesFBref · MLSPA · Google Trends

Wages in professional football are supposed to reflect performance. The more productive you are, the more you earn — as per standard economic theory. But MLS doesn't work that way.

I analyzed 440 outfield players for the 2024 season to answer a straightforward question: are players paid for how they play?

To answer it, I built a dataset combining three sources: detailed performance metrics from FBref (powered by Opta — official data provider of the English Premier League), official MLS salary and roster designation data, and a proxy for public visibility using Google Trends search volume. I then ran multiple fixed-effects panel regressions to isolate which types of performance actually drive wages.

First, the structure

Log salary by roster designation

MLS operates under a salary cap and uses specific roster categories that determine earning potential. Regular roster players are capped at a maximum salary regardless of performance. Homegrown Players — developed through club academies — have similar caps. Designated Players (DPs) are exempt from the cap; clubs can sign up to three at any salary. TAM (Targeted Allocation Money) and U22 Initiative slots allow clubs to pay above the cap within limits. These create wage bands that shape who gets paid and how much.

Unlike European leagues where clubs negotiate wages in an open market, MLS operates as a single-entity system. The league owns all player contracts, and clubs are bound by a salary cap and a roster designation system that determines who can earn what. A defensive midfielder, for example, could play every minute of every game and anchor their team's entire structure, but their wage can't rise above the cap until their contract expires and they are re-designated into a more favorable one.

Four things the market rewards. One it doesn't.

Performance components vs salary

Attacking output matters… a lot. Goals, shots, expected goals — these showed consistent positive associations with salary. Players who score or create high-quality chances earn more, even after controlling for age, experience, position, club budget, etc. Progressive play matters too: carrying the ball forward, completing passes into dangerous areas, advancing play up the pitch all translated into higher wages.

Defensive work? No positive wage premium. Tackles, interceptions, blocks — the metrics that capture defensive contribution had no effect on salary. In some specifications, stronger defensive output was actually associated with lower wages. Granted, defense is about organization and discipline; anticipating and preventing threats before they can even take place; but an odd result nevertheless.

Five positions. Five negative slopes.

Defense score vs salary — by position

MLS rewards what's visible and exciting. What's essential to success but harder to see gets ignored — but that is precisely what wins games in top leagues, and crucially, is rewarded. Just look at the English Premier League, or any of Europe's top 5 leagues for that matter.

The behind-the-scenes midfielder is essential; as seen through the likes of Pedri (FC Barcelona), Elliot Anderson (Nottingham Forest), and Vitinha (Paris Saint-Germain). Quietly orchestrating play, collecting player-of-the-match awards, and being compensated accordingly.

Before, there was Spain and FC Barcelona icon, Sergio Busquets — a founding father of this role. Former Spain manager, Vicente del Bosque, had famously said: "You watch the game, you don't see Busquets. But if you watch Busquets, you see the whole game." Bosque gave the defensive midfielder his debut for Spain in 2009. They would go on to win the World Cup the following year. Busquets "completed football" — winning every major trophy available to him at the club and international levels. Elliot Anderson, Pedri, and Vitinha are young with bright futures. It's clear that this archetype of player is instrumental to success — correlated at the very least. So what's happening in MLS?

The incentives are clear. To maximize earnings, players must focus on what gets noticed: goals, assists, progressive carries — moments that end up on social media. Defensive work — positioning, reading the game, covering for teammates — doesn't create visibility; but visibility is what unlocks better designations in MLS. The unsung heroes tend to remain just that.

The names tell the story

Overall performance vs salary — red = overpaid relative to performance, blue = underpaid

Visibility matters independently of performance. Players with higher public attention — measured through search volume, verified by roster designation — earned more regardless of their on-field output. In fact, it was not uncommon for players in the highest designation tier to be outperformed by those in lower ones.

Designated Players are exempt from the cap — clubs can sign up to three at any salary. These slots go overwhelmingly to attacking players with marquee names. Lorenzo Insigne, Christian Benteke, Lionel Messi… all forwards who made their mark at the highest level before coming to MLS. The DP rule is about who sells tickets and jerseys.

Fame pays — but not how you'd think

Popularity vs salary — by designation

Marginal effect of fame across performance levels

TAM and U22 Initiative slots sit in between; allowing clubs to raise certain wages above the cap within limits. Even here, the slots tend to go to attackers and creative midfielders — players whose contributions are very visible. The patterns observed between performance, popularity, and pay is just how MLS is designed. But there's a reason for that.

MLS is the youngest major sport league in a country where it competes with the likes of NFL, NBA, and MLB for attention, and in a world where everyone has been playing the game for much longer. Before MLS can be world-class, it needs to survive. That means first creating demand: filling stadiums, generating revenue, building a fanbase. MLS recognized that spectacle sells and made a choice: prioritize entertainment and marketability over tactical depth… for now. The roster rules reflect just that. The wage patterns aren't market failures, but the system working as intended.

Which clubs overpay — and which don't

Club wage premium / discount — relative to league average, controlling for squad composition

There's a silver lining though: some clubs have already been figuring out how to exploit the system's inefficiencies. The Vancouver Whitecaps, for example, operate with one of the league's smallest payrolls but remain competitive by doing what the wage structure disincentivizes: investing in defensive organization, tactical discipline, and the subtler aspects of the game. Instead of chasing high-profile attackers, they've built a backline and midfield that can absorb pressure, win second balls, and transition quickly. They develop young talent, use U22 and TAM slots strategically, and rely on structure over star power. It works, and it's efficient.

Players like Sebastian Berhalter show that value exists where the market doesn't price. A defensive midfielder who reads the game, covers ground, and facilitates transitions. The 24 year old American has been vital for the Whitecaps but is not paid like a DP. That gap between performance, popularity, and pay actually presents an opportunity for clubs willing and able to capitalize on it. Perhaps an organic, self-enforcing, steady solution on its own.

The system is not perfectly efficient. There are cracks where strategy and discipline can outperform spending. Clubs that recognize defensive value and chemistry, develop it intelligently, and build tactically coherent systems can compete against bigger budgets. The smartest operators find value where others aren't looking.

What it means

So far, the strategy is working. MLS has grown — new stadiums, bigger broadcast deals, global stars like Messi drawing attention. The league is building the demand. The question now is whether it can build supply.

MLS needs collective quality to be recognized at the highest level. This means rewarding contributions that don't show up in highlight reels: defensive organization, tactical discipline — the work that separates good teams from great ones. Right now, the wage structure doesn't incentivize that. Young defenders and midfielders see limited earning potential unless they move into more attacking roles or other leagues. And even then, attackers that are consistently productive and doing the "right" things have to wait some time before they are able to earn their worth — especially if they are not popular.

The best leagues balance elite attacking talent with strong defensive structures and cohesion alongside a unique identity that defines each club. MLS has the former but underinvests in the latter. The league has options: adjust roster rules to create pathways for high-performing, low-visibility players, shorten the lag between performance and wage recognition, introduce more nuanced performance bonuses. But doing so would mean acknowledging that what built the league might not be what takes it forward. Growth and quality don't always align. MLS bet on growth first. Whether it can shift toward quality without losing what made it grow will define its next chapter.

The exceptions — clubs like Vancouver, players like Berhalter — show another path exists. As the league matures, the question is whether more clubs follow that path, or whether the incentive to chase spectacle continues to dominate. The league's willingness and ability to adapt will define its future. But the future is bright; it is simply a matter of when — not if.
