Something Rotten in the States?
FIFA World Cup '26

Somewhere right now someone is packing a bag for a city they have never been, to watch a team they have supported their whole life. Someone else is setting an alarm for 3am because the group stage draw put their country in a bracket that kicks off at noon in Dallas. Every four years this happens, and it remains, simply, the largest shared experience on the planet. Forty-eight nations. Thirty-two days in which the world is, in some meaningful sense, totally united.

The game has never been better. That claim is worth being precise about, because it is not a vague observation about progress. 66% of all tactical variance across top-flight European football lands on a single axis measuring how completely a team owns a game. Ownership here means exactly that: who controls the ball, the space, the tempo, the terms of the match. Barcelona, Arsenal, Real Madrid, Liverpool each sit at the far end of that axis. Each got there through entirely different means. The data does not reveal football converging on one answer, but rather several, each shaped by a club's resources and identity.

That process over time has produced players who are harder to describe than any previous generation. Lamine Yamal, 18, La Liga Player of the Season two years running, right wing for Barcelona in the sense that his name appears in that column on the team sheet. He led La Liga in assists this season, ranked first in big chances created, second in shots on target, and 293rd in the league for passing accuracy. The numbers do not resolve into a role. Pedri orchestrates and catalyzes Barcelona's rhythm as a spectral midfielder who goes unnoticed for most of the game until he suddenly scores or wins player of the match or both. Fede Valverde: fullback, winger, midfielder. Nico O'Reilly: fullback out of possession, box-crashing attacking midfielder in possession. The list goes on. The game has individuated at the highest level.

And now these individuals arrive at a tournament that reshapes virtually everything their clubs built around them.

Club football is a knowledge system. A pressing trigger is not a rule a player follows but a pattern so rehearsed it precedes conscious thought. The understanding between a midfielder and a striker that produces a run before the pass is played is the product of hundreds of hours of shared experience. International football dismantles that and asks players to reconstruct something in three weeks with people they see a handful of times a year, in service of a national identity that is itself a different kind of instruction than club philosophy.

What the reconstruction produces however is something remarkable. Maradona's Goal of the Century where he ran half the length of the pitch on instinct past five England players because nothing had automated that instinct away. Zidane against Brazil in the 2006 quarter-final was individual intelligence and free-flowing elegance operating without the structure club football builds around it. The reconstruction does not always produce something. But when it does, it produces things clubs really cannot. But this is not to say that there is no structure outside of clubs, but rather, a structure more centered around upbringing. Brazil, France, Spain: Brazilian children, French children, Spanish children. They grew up playing a certain way together and then individuated, developed, and reached their potential with their clubs like a whetstone. And now back with their countries is truly the ultimate playground.

This generation is the most individually distinct in the history of the game. The reconstruction this summer is the most genuinely unknowable in any World Cup. Add a format expanded to 48 teams that gives nations like Morocco and Japan and Senegal more path than they have ever had, and you have a tournament with variance that no supercomputer properly captures. Haaland plays his first World Cup on pitches his father played on in 1994 in the same country. Messi and Ronaldo at their sixth and almost certainly last. Yamal was photographed with Messi in a Camp Nou dressing room at six months old for a UNICEF charity calendar. The handover is happening on the biggest stage it could happen on.

The football and narrative will be extraordinary.

Though something has been accumulating since 2018. Not in the football but around it.

Russia was fine. The football was good. The host was compromised but the tournament ran quite well and most people who went had a reasonable time. Something had shifted, difficult to name, from Brazil the previous tournament hosts.

Qatar was harder to ignore. The tournament was moved to November and December to protect European league schedules and broadcasting contracts, pulled from its natural place in the summer calendar, held in a country whose climate makes outdoor football dangerous. The organizing committee reported 40 worker deaths during construction. Their own secretary-general, speaking off the cuff in an interview, placed the number between 400 and 500. The Guardian's investigation found 6,500 migrant worker deaths across the broader construction period spanning a decade. The football itself was genuinely good. Morocco reached the semi-finals. Messi won what he was owed. Before any of it began, FIFA sent a letter to all 32 competing nations asking them not to bring politics into the tournament.

That sentence is its own indictment.

After Qatar, FIFA awarded 2034 to Saudi Arabia. Human Rights Watch described the scope of risk to the country's 13 million migrant workers as much, much bigger than Qatar. Labor unions are banned. Strikes are banned. Not a single migrant worker was consulted for the supposedly independent human rights assessment FIFA commissioned. Trade unions from 36 countries filed a complaint with the International Labour Organization.

But 2030, Spain, Portugal and Morocco, is genuinely exciting. The first World Cup across two continents. Morocco co-hosting after their 2022 run changed how the world sees African football. No immediate concerns. Just football in places that love football.

The sequence from 2018 to 2034 is not a straight line. It has a break in it, but the pattern is curious and not easy to overlook. Like a zebra with horizontal stripes. It's still a zebra, but is it really?

The last time the United States hosted was 1994. To understand what that tournament meant, you have to understand what the country was that summer.

The Cold War had just ended. For nearly half a century, American life had been organized around an enemy, and then suddenly the enemy was gone. What replaced it was not triumphalism exactly, but something quieter and stranger: the sense that the big questions had been settled. Francis Fukuyama had published The End of History two years earlier, and whether or not anyone actually read it, the argument became the feeling of the moment. Liberal democracy had won. Markets had won. There was nothing left to fight about, only progress to be managed.

And the country relaxed into it. The economy was growing again after a recession. The internet existed but barely, more rumor than presence. The culture was loud and confident and a little ridiculous. People had jobs, bought homes, watched the same shows, argued about the same movies. There was a shared center to American life that is difficult to describe now without sounding nostalgic, but it was real, and it was measurable: the wealthiest families were 42 times richer than middle-class families. A large gap. But a gap across which people could still see each other.

Into that country came the World Cup. America barely knew the sport. It didn't matter. The average ticket cost $58, and Alan Rothenberg, who ran the tournament, told a press conference that a $25 ticket was within reach of most working people and families. He was right, and they came. Three-point-six million people, still the highest attendance in World Cup history, in a country that needed the offside rule explained to it. The tournament worked because the country it landed in was open, in mood and in fact. The condition of the award was that the United States create a professional league. MLS launched in 1996. The whole thing was a bet on openness, and the bet paid.

Thirty-two years later, the same Alan Rothenberg sits on the 2026 Los Angeles host committee. He describes the contracts governing host city responsibilities as very, very one-sided.

The country he is describing them from is a different one. Not poorer. Much richer, in aggregate. But the aggregate is the problem. The wealthiest families are now 71 times richer than the middle class. Median income has not moved in real terms since 2019. The shared center that made a $25 ticket meaningful has thinned, and the tournament's pricing reflects the country it is actually in rather than the one it was in before: when the US submitted its bid in 2018, the maximum projected price for a final ticket was $1,550. Dynamic pricing has since carried it to $10,990. The train to the stadium costs eleven times its normal fare on match days.

And the openness, the thing 1994 was actually a bet on, has inverted. Travel bans cover 39 countries. Fans from qualifying nations face visa bonds of up to $15,000. Haiti and Iran are banned outright: players can come, supporters cannot. Amnesty International issued a travel advisory for the host country of the World Cup. That sentence has not been written before.

Same man. Same tournament. The difference is the country around it.

To understand what has happened to the World Cup as an economic object, it helps to understand what the World Cup actually produces.

It produces a feeling. The feeling of the world gathering. That is not a sentimental description. It is a precise one. Broadcasters pay billions for the right to transmit it. Sponsors pay billions for proximity to it. The final ticket finds buyers at nearly $11,000 because the people buying it understand, correctly, that what they are purchasing is access to something that cannot be replicated or substituted. The World Cup is a captive market in the most literal sense: the product is unique and the people who want it have nowhere else to go.

What creates that feeling is not the stadiums or the broadcast infrastructure or the organizational capacity of FIFA. It is the fans. The supporter who saved two years to follow his national team. The diaspora community that turns a section of an American stadium into somewhere else entirely for ninety minutes. The neutral who watches because the whole world is watching and that fact alone means something. These people are not incidental to the product FIFA sells. They are the product. Remove them and there are 104 games of football, well-organized and broadcast to large audiences.

That is not the same thing.

FIFA will collect between nine and thirteen billion dollars from this tournament. Broadcasting rights alone account for $3.92 billion, up 22% from Qatar. American brands now account for 52% of sponsorship revenue, up from 36% four years ago. The expansion to 48 teams and 104 games was a revenue decision. FIFA projected a 20% increase in commercial income from it.

Host cities collect none of it. No ticket revenue, no concessions, no parking, no merchandise. The contracts were signed before bids were submitted. Many remain secret. The United States government is spending $625 million of taxpayer money on security. Victor Matheson, an economist who has studied mega-events for decades, called FIFA's own $40 billion projected economic impact a press release, not research. His calculation: the numbers imply each of the 104 games generates $400 million, roughly $5,000 to $7,000 per fan in attendance.

The academic literature on why cities bid regardless is clear. Competitive bidding inflates perceived value beyond realistic return. Governments systematically over-forecast economic benefit from large events. Political incentives reward officials for the prestige of hosting at costs borne by taxpayers who had no vote on the terms. The organizing committee represents itself, not the city.

This is the economic structure of rent-seeking. FIFA did not create the value it extracts. The game created it. A century of players and fans and nations created it. FIFA positions itself between that value and the people who want access to it, and collects.

The deeper problem is what economists would recognize as a commons tragedy. The atmosphere of the World Cup is a shared resource. Everyone extracts from it and nobody reproduces it. FIFA monetizes it. Sponsors buy access to it. Host nations leverage it for geopolitical prestige. The fans who create it are priced out financially and excluded legally. The stadiums still fill. The broadcast numbers hold for now. But the thing that made the World Cup irreplaceable, the genuine sense of the world gathered rather than approximated, thins quietly. By the time it is fully gone, everyone will have already adjusted to its absence. That is what a commons looks like when it runs down.

There is a further mechanism at work. When the price of a final ticket functions not as a reflection of what the event costs to stage but as a signal of exclusivity, the product changes category. A luxury World Cup and the World Cup are not the same thing. The original derives its irreplaceable quality from universality. It belongs to everyone, which is why it can stop wars and move governments and generate the specific feeling that makes a broadcast rights deal worth $3.92 billion. A luxury version of it is a very expensive sports event. Expensive sports events are, unlike universal ones, substitutable. Substitutable things are fragile in ways universal things are not.

Eighty percent of hotel bookings across host cities are running below projections. Up to 70% of FIFA-reserved rooms in major host cities have been cancelled. The stadiums will fill. The tournament will run. But the fans filling those stadiums will be primarily domestic Americans who bought tickets because this is the thing to do this summer. The world will watch on screens. The gathering, in the physical sense that gives the product its value, will be partial.

Chuck Blazer kept two apartments in Trump Tower. One for himself, one for his cats. Blazer was the FIFA and CONCACAF official who became an FBI informant and helped expose the largest corruption scandal in football history. FBI agents confronted him outside the building in November 2011. His cooperation led to the arrest of seven FIFA officials in Zurich in May 2015, the most consequential accountability moment in the sport's governance history.

In July 2025, FIFA opened its new United States offices in Trump Tower.

The building that housed the man who brought FIFA down is now FIFA's building. Ten years. Same address. Gianni Infantino attended Trump's inauguration, toured the Middle East with him, arrived hours late to FIFA's annual congress because he was traveling with the president. He brought the Club World Cup trophy to the Oval Office. Chelsea, the actual winners, had to lift a replica because Trump had the original on his desk. Infantino invented a FIFA Peace Prize after Trump expressed displeasure at not receiving the Nobel, and awarded it to him at the World Cup draw at the Kennedy Center in December. An ethics complaint followed: Infantino had given clear support for Trump's political agenda in a way that posed a clear threat to the integrity and reputation of football and of FIFA itself.

None of this touches the football.

The game keeps improving, indifferent to who administers it and indifferent to what the train costs. What the players produce on the pitch will be worth watching regardless of everything around it.

But the World Cup has always been more than the football on the pitch. It has been the feeling that what is happening matters to the whole world equally, that for thirty-two days the planet shares something. That feeling depends on conditions. On who can afford to attend. On who is permitted to travel. On whether the institution running the tournament and the country hosting it understand what they are actually in charge of.

What the World Cup becomes when the atmosphere that gives it value is no longer reproducible is a question the extraction model does not ask.

The Groups

Group A

Mexico (#15): Sixteen World Cup appearances and a football culture built on technical, organized play with strong midfield quality and attacking intent. A quarter-final ceiling that has never been broken, including twice as hosts. Competitive enough to win individual matches against strong opposition without yet showing the capacity to sustain that quality across a full tournament.

South Korea (#25): Twelve consecutive World Cup appearances, the longest active streak outside the traditional powers. Football built on collective discipline, high pressing, and direct attacking play. The 2002 semi-final on home soil remains the benchmark. A competitive Asian side with genuine quality at its peak.

South Africa (#60): Hosted the 2010 tournament, the most significant moment in African football's history at this level. Back at a World Cup for the first time since.

Czechia (#41): A physically organized European side built on defensive structure, set pieces, and aerial dominance. Hard to beat in a single match.

Group B

Canada (#30): First World Cup in 40 years, built through three decades of deliberate investment in football infrastructure. A generation now producing top-level talent for European clubs. No historical identity at this tournament. This generation is building one.

Switzerland (#19): One of the most consistent major tournament nations of the past fifteen years without ever threatening to win one. Defensively organized, compact, and genuinely hard to break down. A quarter-final in 2022 is the high point of the modern era.

Bosnia-Herzegovina (#65): One previous World Cup in 2014. Through the UEFA playoffs and at the end of a generation rather than the beginning.

Qatar (#55): Hosted 2022 and became the worst-performing host nation in the tournament's history, exiting without a point. Back through AFC qualification.

Group C

Brazil (#6): Five World Cup titles and 24 years without one, a gap that defines everything about the pressure on every Brazilian squad. The historical identity is jogo bonito, creative and expressive attacking football built on freedom and individual expression, and every generation since 2002 has been measured against that memory. Under a European manager for the first time in the modern era, the current side has genuine attacking depth and a defensive foundation among the strongest in the tournament. Whether this generation can play with the freedom the country demands has been the central question for over two decades.

Morocco (#8): The 2022 semi-final was the first by an African nation in the tournament's history, achieved through a specific and hard-won collective identity: organized defensive mid-block, synchronized positional movement, and clinical transition. Three months before this tournament the manager who built that identity left following a controversial AFCON final defeat at home. His replacement enters his first senior role. Key attacking contributors from 2022 are absent. The defensive foundation remains. The settled collective identity does not.

Scotland (#43): Nine World Cup appearances and a group stage exit every single time. A 28-year absence ended dramatically on the final night of qualifying. The best Scottish squad in the modern era.

Haiti (#83): Qualified under extreme domestic conditions. Most supporters cannot attend. Their presence is its own achievement.

Group D

United States (#16): Hosted in 1994 and reached the quarter-final in 2002. Three decades of infrastructure and development have produced the most European-based American generation in the sport's history. A clear collective structure under the current manager. Playing at home.

Turkey (#22): Finished third at the 2002 World Cup. The most internationally connected Turkish generation ever produced, with genuine world-class creative quality at the highest level of European club football. A fluid attacking system without a reliable finisher. Capable of extraordinary performances and poor ones across the same campaign.

Paraguay (#40): First World Cup in 16 years. CONMEBOL-hardened, physically imposing, defensively organized. Reached the quarter-final in 2010.

Australia (#27): Reached the quarter-final in 2022. A reputation for competing beyond their ranking through organization and defensive commitment.

Group E

Germany (#10): Four World Cup titles, the last in 2014. Group stage exits in 2018 and 2022 forced a reckoning. Rebuilt around gegenpressing and creative attacking football, arriving on a nine-game winning streak. The structural vulnerability of space behind the high defensive line in transition has not been eliminated.

Ivory Coast (#34): Won AFCON in 2023. Historically one of African football's most technically gifted nations, with a long tradition of direct, creative attacking play. Beat France 2-1 in a warmup five days before the tournament, their historic first win over Les Bleus, coming from behind.

Ecuador (#23): CONMEBOL-hardened, organized, competitive. Reached the quarter-final in 2006.

Curaçao (#82): The smallest nation by population ever to qualify for a World Cup. The achievement of being here is the story.

Group F

Netherlands (#7): Three World Cup finals and no title. Total Football shaped the modern game and the Netherlands shaped Total Football. The current squad arrives with injury absences in key attacking and defensive positions. Genuine defensive authority and midfield quality remain. The chronic absence of a clinical central striker is a structural problem that has followed Dutch squads for a decade.

Japan (#18): The most systematic development story in Asian football. Two decades building a collective identity around organized pressing, vertical transition, and disciplined defensive shape. Beat Germany and Spain in 2022 from that same identity. The most European-experienced Japanese squad ever assembled, arriving without several key attacking contributors through injury.

Sweden (#38): A well-organized defensive structure with elite attacking quality at the top and limited depth around it. Fourth place in 1994 is the high point.

Tunisia (#44): Compact and disciplined. Their 1978 win over Mexico was the first by an African nation at a World Cup.

Group G

Belgium (#9): The golden generation that promised a major title and delivered third place at the 2018 World Cup. The squad has aged considerably at its most important positions, arriving with limited recent club football for several key figures. Creative midfield quality at the highest level remains genuine alongside a younger generation beginning to assert itself.

Egypt (#29): Last at a World Cup in 1990, ending a 36-year absence. A technically capable side whose attacking quality is concentrated at the top of the squad with limited depth behind.

Iran (#21): Consistent AFC qualifier, defensively organized, and hard to score against across multiple World Cup campaigns. Never advanced past the group stage.

New Zealand (#85): The tournament's lowest-ranked side and the OFC's sole representative. Physically organized and capable of competing across ninety minutes.

Group H

Spain (#2): Won the World Cup in 2010 and Euro 2024. The tournament favourite at 16.1% by the Opta supercomputer and the only side rated more likely than not to reach the quarter-finals. Built on a possession philosophy that shaped modern football, now combined with more direct wide attacking movement. The defensive foundation was fully restored in March. Tournament-ready.

Uruguay (#17): Won the World Cup in 1930 and 1950. A football culture built on collective toughness, defensive hardness, and a century of competing above its size. Under Bielsa that identity has been combined with high-intensity pressing and direct vertical attacking play without losing its essential character. Defensive and midfield quality at the highest level of European club football.

Saudi Arabia (#61): Beat Argentina in 2022 through a specific organized pressing structure under a manager who had built that identity with the squad over years. That manager was dismissed two months before this tournament. The tactical identity that produced that result is gone.

Cape Verde (#69): First World Cup appearance for a nation of half a million people.

Group I

France (#1): Won the World Cup in 1998 and 2018. The historical identity blends physical organization with explosive attacking quality in a system that deploys extraordinary collective talent rather than depending on any single figure. The deepest attacking squad at this tournament, assembled for the manager's farewell after 14 years. Ivory Coast beat them 2-1 five days ago. The manager called it a reminder.

Senegal (#14): Won their first AFCON in 2022 and reached the quarter-final at their debut World Cup in 2002. Physical, high-pressing football built on wide attacking overloads and a powerful defensive foundation. The squad is at its most experienced and collectively complete, though the AFCON final on home soil, a controversial defeat followed by a walkoff protest and CAF investigation, adds turbulence going in.

Norway (#31): Four consecutive World Cups between 1986 and 1998, including a group stage win over Brazil, followed by a 28-year absence. Direct, counter-attacking football with extraordinary individual quality at the top and limited creative depth around it. Group I with France and Senegal is the hardest possible draw.

Iraq (#57): Last World Cup in 1986. A 40-year absence ends here.

Group J

Argentina (#3): Three World Cup titles, in 1978, 1986, and 2022. Defending champions. The historical identity is tactical flexibility, collective resilience, and an institutional knowledge of how to win tournaments when not playing their best football. No team has retained the World Cup since Brazil in 1962.

Algeria (#28): Technically gifted, built on organized defensive shape and quick transition through European-based attacking talent. Their 2014 round of 16, pushing eventual champions Germany to extra time, is the high watermark. Genuine midfield authority and attacking depth in the current squad.

Austria (#24): Under Rangnick, one of the most coherent pressing systems in European international football. Organized, intensive, and difficult to break down. Haven't been to a World Cup since 1998.

Jordan (#63): First World Cup appearance in the nation's history.

Group K

Portugal (#5): A technically gifted football culture with a long tradition of producing creative, individually brilliant players. Third place in 1966, Euro 2016 winners, no World Cup title. The current squad has genuine creative and technical depth. The tactical framework built around an ageing figurehead constrains how freely that quality can express itself, and that tension remains unresolved.

Colombia (#13): A tradition of creative, attacking South American football. Quarter-final in 2014 is the best World Cup result. Genuine depth in European club football with dangerous attacking quality in wide areas.

Uzbekistan (#50): First World Cup, 35 years after independence. Managed by a 2006 World Cup winner. Most of the squad plays in the domestic league. The significance of a first appearance is its own category.

Congo DR (#46): Last World Cup in 1974 as Zaire. A 52-year absence ends here.

Group L

England (#4): The birthplace of football and one World Cup title, in 1966. A long pattern of underachievement relative to talent, though the current qualifying record suggests genuine structural improvement. Attacking depth and squad quality among the best in the tournament.

Croatia (#11): A nation of four million people with a World Cup final in 2018 and a semi-final in 2022. The identity is endurance rather than dominance. Defensively intelligent, collectively organized, and significantly harder to eliminate in knockout football than their ranking suggests. The squad is older. The identity has not changed.

Ghana (#74): Reached the quarter-final in 2010. Direct, pace-based attacking football with quality in wide areas. The current squad arrives with significant injuries to key figures and a manager who replaced his predecessor weeks before the tournament.

Panama (#33): Second World Cup. CONCACAF qualifier.
