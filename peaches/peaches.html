The Market for Peaches

In 1970, George Akerlof published "The Market for Lemons." On a used-car lot there are lemons and there are peaches, bad cars and good ones, and only the seller knows which is which. The buyer, unable to tell, offers the average price of a car on the lot; the owners of peaches pull their cars rather than sell good ones at that price, the average quality falls, the price follows it down, and the lot converges to lemons. Markets respond with guarantees, brands, and certification. Akerlof would win a Nobel prize for his work.

Labor runs the same experiment, except peaches cannot really exit; the best they can do is sell at the lemon price when possible. To see how that plays out, I analyzed a decade of BLS employment projections, CPS employment by age, AI-exposure scores for 756 occupations, and current Federal Reserve research. The chain, 2024–34:

Fewer births since 2008 means fewer young workers: the 16–24 labor force falls 10% this decade while the 75+ labor force grows 70%.

Labor force change by age, 2024–34

Employment can only outgrow the labor force while unemployment is falling, and that tailwind is spent. Next decade both grow 0.3% a year.

What demand remains follows the aging population. Care and computation grow, clerical work dies, and a striking share of the growth verifies: security analysts +28.5%, actuaries +21.8%, financial examiners +18.5%.

Employment change by industry, 2024–34

The growth sits behind experience. Degree jobs gated by prior work grow a quarter faster, pay a quarter better ($104,690 vs $81,680), and employ almost nobody under 25.

The entry door has closed. Remote work made juniors expensive to teach, which the New York Fed says explains 64% of the rise in young-graduate unemployment, and AI makes them unnecessary to have.

Skill groups: AI exposure × projected growth (bubble = occupations)

The bill lands on entrants: 5.7% unemployment against 4.3% for all workers, 41% underemployment, earnings scars that run a decade, and fewer children. Loop back to the beginning.

Akerlof's cures resurface, funded this time by the worker. Guarantees become probation and contract-first work, brands mean the school or the first employer or a staked referral, and certification is the license, which works because it contains the supervised practice.

Meanwhile 27% of the gated workforce is 55 or older and leaving. Buying beats making until it doesn't; then firms train again. And a peach at a lemon price is arbitrage, which makes the natural buyer small, new, and willing to verify people by working beside them.

The queue outside the gate is the entire mid-career supply of the 2030s, the smallest cohort in decades. When replacement hiring comes, it comes as a bidding war for the people the market spent a decade discounting.
